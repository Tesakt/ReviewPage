Reviews of stories

The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August

by Claire North


In "The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August," Harry August is a man who is born again and again with the knowledge of his previous lives. He is an Ouroboran, also known as a kalachakra, and is destined to repeat his life over and over again.

The story begins in 1919, when Harry is born in the women's washroom of Berwick-upon-Tweed station. He leads an unremarkable life and dies in hospital in Newcastle-upon-Tyne in 1989. But then, he finds himself reborn again in 1919, gaining the knowledge of his earlier life at an early age.

As he grows older, Harry learns that he is not alone and is soon contacted by the Cronus Club, an organization of similarly affected individuals. The Cronus Club looks after Harry in his childhood in subsequent lives, and he eventually goes on to study biology, chemistry, and physics. With knowledge from his previous lives, he easily becomes a professor of physics at the University of Cambridge, where he meets Vincent Rankis, an intelligent undergraduate student.

Vincent and Harry become friends and talk about theoretical physics and the nature of time. Eventually, they both realize that they are Ouroborans and must confront the impending end of the world and the role they play in its destruction. Other members of the Cronus Club inform Harry that the world is ending and that with each life he lives, the end becomes closer.

Harry sets out on a quest to find the source of the impending apocalypse and prevent it from happening. Along the way, he must confront the consequences of his actions and the ways in which his choices can shape the world around him.

One of the things I loved most about this book was the way it explored the concept of time and the nature of reality. The idea that Harry is doomed to repeat his life over and over again raises all sorts of interesting questions about determinism, free will, and the interconnectedness of all things. The book is also a subtle study of friendship, love, and the fluid complexity of existence.

Overall, I highly recommend "The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August" to anyone who enjoys science fiction and speculative fiction. It's a thought-provoking and engaging novel that will keep you turning the pages long into the night. Give it a read and see for yourself!
